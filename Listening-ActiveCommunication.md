# Listening and Active Communication

## 1. What are the steps/strategies to do Active Listening? (Minimum 6 points)

- Try to be more attentive.

- Try to be a part of the conversation by nodding and using small responses like “okay” or “hm.”

- Try to make notes of important points in the conversation.

- Try to ask important questions in a polite and respectful way.

- Try to maintain a good posture so the speaker feels you are not bored.

- Try to make eye contact and listen carefully.

---

## 2. According to Fisher's model, what are the key points of Reflective Listening? (Write in your own words, use simple English)

- You have to focus fully on the other person. No need to interupt, judge or plan your reply while they are talking.

- You repeat back the main idea in your own words. This helps the speaker to know that you heard them correctly.

- Try to understand the feelings, not just the words.

- No need to jump in with solutions. Give the person space to express themselves fully.

- Try to be patient and calm. Let the speaker take time to think and talk.

- Try to make sure what you have understood is correct.

- Try to see the situation from the speaker's view. Let them feel respected and understood.

---

## 3. What are the obstacles in your listening process?

- Getting distracted easily because of the noise, movement, or even your own thoughts can pull your attention away from the speaker.

- Instead of listening, you start planning what you want to say next.

- If you feel angry, stressed, tired, or upset, it becomes harder to listen properly.

- Anyone can form an opinion before hearing the full message.

- Suppose, If the topic doesn't feel important or engaging, your mind may wander. It shows lack of interest.


## 4. What can you do to improve your listening?

- Stay focused on their words and keep your mind in the present moment.

- Let the speaker share their thoughts fully before you respond.

- Try not to disrespect the speaker while they are conveying something.

- Try to maintain good eye contact and body posture because this shows that you care about what they are saying.

- Don't ask questions to challenge or argue.

- Try to practise active learning because giving small resonses like "okay", "I see", or nod your head this will show you are follwing.

## 5. When do you switch to Passive communication style in your day-to-day life?

- I generally switch to a passive communication style when I don't want to create conflict.

- Sometimes, I stay quiet when i disagree because I don't want to hurt someone's feelings.

- I become passive when I feel nervous, shy, or afraid that my opinion might be judged.

- In group settings, I may avoid sharing my ideas because I think others might not listen.

---

## 6. When do you switch into Aggressive communication style in your day-to-day life?

- I switch to an aggressive communication style whenever I feel stressed or frustrated.

- Sometimes, if someone does not try to understand that they are wrong and starts acting oversmart, I raise my voice a little.

- I don't speak in a harsh or hurtful way, but my tone becomes firm and slightly louder because I feel frustrated in that moment.

---

## 7. When do you switch into Passive-Aggressive communication style in your day-to-day life?

- I switch to passive-aggressive communication when I don’t want to speak directly about a problem, especially when the other person is ignoring it.

- Sometimes, I give silent treatment instead of explaining how I feel when someone disrespects me.

- Whenever I feel that expressing my feelings might create trouble, I hide them and show my irritation indirectly.

- When I feel hurt, I sometimes use sarcasm because I don’t want to say my feelings openly.

## 8. How can you make your communication assertive? You can watch and analyse the videos, then think what would be a few steps you can apply in your own life? (Watch the videos first before answering this question.)

- Notice and understand your feelings and needs before speaking.

- Maintain confident body language: good posture, calm gestures, and eye contact.

- Speak up at the right time instead of holding things in.

- Use “I” statements to express your thoughts without blaming others.

- Listen actively and let others finish before you respond.

- Set clear boundaries when needed and say “no” politely but firmly.

- Stay calm even if the situation is tense, and focus on solutions.
